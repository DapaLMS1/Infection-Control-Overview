Infection Control Overview

Your Role in Infection Prevention and Control

As an Apprentice Dental Assistant, you have a fundamental role in preventing the spread of infection within the dental practice. Upholding high standards of infection control is essential for the safety of patients and the entire dental team. This guide outlines the key areas of the practice where you must apply correct infection prevention and control principles, in line with national standards.

Process: Between-Patient Chair & Lamp Disinfection

  1. Wear PPE: Put on utility gloves, mask, and protective eyewear.
  2. Clean First: Use a TGA-approved detergent wipe to physically clean all surfaces. Start with the overhead lamp (handles, switches, shield), then move to the chair itself, cleaning from least contaminated areas (e.g., armrests) to most contaminated (e.g., headrest, backrest). Ensure all visible debris is removed.
  3. Disinfect Second: Use a TGA-approved hospital-grade disinfectant wipe. Thoroughly wipe all cleaned surfaces again, ensuring the surface remains wet for the contact time specified by the manufacturer.
  4. Dry: Allow surfaces to air dry completely. Do not wipe them dry.
  5. Place Barriers: Once dry, place fresh, single-use barriers on high-touch areas like the lamp handles, switches, headrest, and chair control buttons.

Process: Cleaning Surfaces and Drawers

  1. Identify Clinical Contact Surfaces: This includes the bracket table, countertops, and drawer handles.
  2. Clean and Disinfect: Using the same 'clean then disinfect' principle as the chair, work systematically across all surfaces. Pay close attention to drawer handles and the edges of countertops.
  3. Manage Drawers: Avoid opening drawers with contaminated gloves. Use cotton pliers or over-gloves if you must access items during treatment. Regularly audit and clean the inside of drawers as per practice policy.

Process: Waste Segregation

  • Sharps Waste: Immediately dispose of all used needles, blades, and anaesthetic cartridges into a puncture-resistant sharps container located at the point of use. Never overfill the container.
  • Contaminated (Clinical) Waste: Place items visibly soiled with blood or saliva (gauze, cotton rolls) into a biohazard-lined bin. Securely tie the bag when it is three-quarters full.
  • General Waste: Non-contaminated items like paper packaging can go into the general waste bin.

Process: Cleaning Clinical Technology

Keyboards, mice, and monitors are high-touch surfaces that can harbour pathogens. They require careful cleaning to prevent damage.

  • Keyboard: If a waterproof, washable keyboard is not used, a protective barrier must be placed over it and changed between patients. To clean, disconnect the keyboard, turn it upside down to dislodge debris, and wipe all surfaces with a TGA-approved disinfectant wipe suitable for electronics.
  • Mouse: The mouse must be covered with a single-use barrier that is changed between patients. After removing the barrier, wipe the entire surface with a TGA-approved disinfectant wipe suitable for electronics.
  • Monitor: Use a screen-safe cleaning wipe or a soft, lint-free cloth lightly dampened with a recommended screen cleaner. Avoid spraying liquid directly onto the screen. If it is a touch screen, it must be barrier-protected and disinfected between patients.

Process: Instrument Cleaning

  1. Wear Heavy-Duty PPE: This includes utility gloves, a fluid-resistant gown, a mask, and a face shield.
  2. Manual or Mechanical Cleaning: If cleaning manually, fully immerse instruments in a TGA-approved detergent solution and scrub below the waterline to prevent aerosols. Alternatively, place instruments in a cassette and process through an ultrasonic cleaner or thermal washer-disinfector.
  3. Rinse and Dry: Thoroughly rinse all instruments with running water and dry them completely using a lint-free cloth or a drying cabinet.
  4. Inspect: Carefully inspect each instrument for any remaining debris or damage. If not clean, repeat the process.

Process: Instrument Packaging

  1. Choose Correct Pouch Size: Select a pouch that allows the instrument to fit without being cramped, but is not excessively large.
  2. Insert Instrument & Indicator: Place the clean, dry instrument into the pouch. Add an internal chemical indicator to verify sterilant penetration.
  3. Seal Securely: For self-sealing pouches, ensure a complete, wrinkle-free seal. For heat-sealed pouches, follow the manufacturer's instructions for the correct temperature and pressure.
  4. Label the Pouch: Label each pouch with the steriliser number, cycle number, and date of sterilisation.

Process: Sterilisation Cycle and Storage

  1. Load the Steriliser (Autoclave): Place pouches on their edge in racks, separating paper from plastic to allow steam circulation. Do not overload the chamber.
  2. Run Cycle: Select the appropriate cycle for the load and start the steriliser.
  3. Verify and Document: At the end of the cycle, verify the physical parameters (time, temperature, pressure) have been met. Check that the external chemical indicator has changed colour. Record the cycle details in the sterilisation log.
  4. Store Correctly: Once cooled and dried, store sterile packs in a clean, dry, enclosed area, away from potential contamination.

Process: Routine Surface Cleaning

A schedule should be in place for regularly cleaning high-touch surfaces throughout the day.

  • Checklist: Door handles, reception counters, pens, EFTPOS terminals, and armrests on chairs.
  • Method: Use a general-purpose detergent wipe or solution to clean these surfaces. A disinfectant may be used as per practice policy, especially during periods of high community illness.

Process: Managing Amenities

  • Hand Sanitiser: Ensure alcohol-based hand rub is available and clearly visible for patient use upon entry. Check and refill the dispenser regularly.
  • Reading Materials/Toys: These items are difficult to clean effectively. It is best practice to remove them. If kept, they must be part of a regular cleaning schedule.
  • Signage: Display posters promoting respiratory hygiene ('catch it, bin it, kill it') and hand hygiene.

Process: Identifying and Managing Ill Patients

  • Screening: Reception staff should be trained to politely ask screening questions if a patient presents with obvious symptoms of a respiratory illness (e.g., coughing).
  • Reschedule: If a patient is unwell, it is safest for them, other patients, and staff to reschedule their non-emergency appointment.
  • Isolation: If an unwell patient must be seen, they should be asked to wear a mask, perform hand hygiene, and be moved to an empty surgery or isolated area as quickly as possible.
